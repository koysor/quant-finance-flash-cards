# Flash Card Style Guide

All cards generated by this skill MUST follow this structure and tone to ensure consistency with the project's card system.

## Metadata and Tone
- **Header:** Concept name as a Level 1 heading (`# Name`).
- **Metadata:** Topic, Tags, Created, and Author immediately below.
- **Tone:** Clear, succinct, and educational.
- **Audience:** A Level maths standard student.
- **Language:** British English (e.g., "normalised", "optimisation", "favour").
- **Mathematics:** Use LaTeX. Inline: `$a^2$`. Block: `$$ ... $$`.

## Examples

### Return on Investment
# Return on Investment

**Topic:** Financial Mathematics
**Tags:** returns, performance, profit
**Created:** 2026-04-19
**Author:** Gemini CLI

---

## Definition
The **relative** change in the value of an asset over a period — not the absolute profit in currency. It allows for the comparison of performance across assets of different sizes.

## Key Formula

$$R = \frac{S_1 - S_0 + D}{S_0}$$

Where:
- $S_0$ = starting price
- $S_1$ = ending price
- $D$ = cash dividends or coupons received during the period

## Example
A stock is bought for £50 and sold for £55 a year later, having paid a £1 dividend.
$$R = \frac{55 - 50 + 1}{50} = \frac{6}{50} = 12\%$$

## Remember
Using **returns** (rather than raw price changes) is the fundamental starting point for almost all quantitative modeling. It normalises data, allowing a £5 stock and a £500 stock to be compared on equal footing.

### Portfolio Weights
# Portfolio Weights

**Topic:** Portfolio Theory & Asset Pricing
**Tags:** weights, allocation, constraint
**Created:** 2026-04-19
**Author:** Gemini CLI

---

## Definition
**Portfolio weights** express each asset's share of total portfolio value. They are the fundamental language of portfolio construction — every allocation decision comes down to choosing weights.

## Key Formula
The weight $w_i$ for asset $i$ in a portfolio of $N$ assets is:
$$w_i = \frac{\text{Market Value of Asset } i}{\sum_{j=1}^N \text{Market Value of Asset } j}$$

The budget constraint requires:
$$\sum_{i=1}^{N} w_i = 1$$

## Example
A portfolio worth £100k holds £60k in equity and £40k in bonds:
$$w_{\text{equity}} = 0.6, \quad w_{\text{bonds}} = 0.4, \quad 0.6 + 0.4 = 1 \checkmark$$

## Remember
Weights sum to 1 — this single constraint is known as the budget equation. In quantitative finance, short positions are represented by negative weights ($w_i < 0$), while leverage is shown by weights greater than 1 ($w_i > 1$).
