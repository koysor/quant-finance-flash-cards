## Definition

**Parametric equations** express the coordinates $(x, y)$ of a curve as separate functions of an auxiliary variable $t$ called the **parameter**, rather than as a direct relationship $y = f(x)$. They are used when a single equation in $x$ and $y$ would be awkward or multi-valued (e.g. circles, ellipses, spirals).

## Key Formula

A parametric curve: $x = f(t)$, $y = g(t)$ for $t$ in some range.

**Eliminating the parameter:** solve one equation for $t$ and substitute into the other to find the Cartesian form $y = h(x)$.

**Common example — circle of radius $r$:**

$$x = r\cos t, \quad y = r\sin t, \quad t \in [0, 2\pi] \implies x^2 + y^2 = r^2$$

## Example

A particle moves so that $x = t^2 - 1$ and $y = 2t$. Find the Cartesian equation of its path.

From the second equation: $t = y/2$. Substituting:

$$x = \left(\frac{y}{2}\right)^2 - 1 = \frac{y^2}{4} - 1 \implies y^2 = 4(x + 1)$$

This is a parabola with vertex at $(-1, 0)$.

## Remember

Parametric equations are the mathematical structure of **Monte Carlo simulation**. An asset price path $S(t)$ generated by $S_{t+\Delta t} = S_t \cdot e^{(\mu - \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2)\Delta t + \sigma\sqrt{\Delta t}\,Z_i}$ is a parametric curve in the $(t, S)$ plane — the parameter $t$ drives the evolution, and each realised path is a different parametric curve. Every Monte Carlo scenario is a parametric trajectory, and the simulation reports properties of the distribution of these curves. Understanding parametric equations is prerequisite for understanding path-dependent option pricing.
