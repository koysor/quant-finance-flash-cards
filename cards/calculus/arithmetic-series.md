# Arithmetic Series

**Tags:** arithmetic series, sum, common difference, sequences, annuity, coupon, bonds

## Definition

An **arithmetic series** is the sum of an arithmetic sequence — a sequence in which each term increases by a fixed constant $d$ called the **common difference**. The $n$th term is $a_n = a + (n-1)d$ where $a$ is the first term.

## Key Formula

**Sum of first $n$ terms:**

$$S_n = \frac{n}{2}(2a + (n-1)d) = \frac{n}{2}(a + l)$$

where $a$ is the first term, $d$ is the common difference, and $l = a + (n-1)d$ is the last term.

**Sum of first $n$ natural numbers** (special case $a = 1$, $d = 1$):

$$1 + 2 + \cdots + n = \frac{n(n+1)}{2}$$

## Example

A graduate's salary starts at £30,000 and increases by £2,000 per year. What are total earnings over 5 years?

$a = 30{,}000$, $d = 2{,}000$, $n = 5$.

$$S_5 = \frac{5}{2}(2 \times 30{,}000 + 4 \times 2{,}000) = \frac{5}{2}(60{,}000 + 8{,}000) = \frac{5}{2} \times 68{,}000 = £170{,}000$$

## Remember

Arithmetic series arise in **level payment loans** and **coupon accrual schedules**. When the accrued interest on a bond increases by the same amount each day between coupon dates (assuming a simple interest convention), the total accrued interest over $n$ days is an arithmetic series. In **amortisation tables** — where each period's interest charge decreases by a fixed amount as the outstanding balance falls — the sum of all interest payments is an arithmetic series, letting the total financing cost be calculated instantly without summing row by row.
