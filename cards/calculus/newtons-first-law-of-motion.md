# Newton's First Law of Motion

**Topic:** Calculus
**Tags:** newton, inertia, momentum, mechanics, kinematics
**Author:** Claude Sonnet 4.6

---

## Definition

Newton's First Law states that a body remains at rest, or continues moving at constant velocity in a straight line, unless acted upon by a resultant external force. This resistance to changes in motion is called *inertia*.

## Key Formula

Zero resultant force means zero acceleration:

$$\sum \mathbf{F} = \mathbf{0} \implies \mathbf{a} = \mathbf{0}$$

Equivalently, the rate of change of linear momentum $\mathbf{p} = m\mathbf{v}$ is zero:

$$\frac{d\mathbf{p}}{dt} = \mathbf{0} \implies \mathbf{v} = \text{constant}$$

## Example

A 5 kg block rests on a frictionless surface with two horizontal forces applied: $F_1 = 8\text{ N}$ to the right and $F_2 = 8\text{ N}$ to the left. The resultant force is $\sum F = 8 - 8 = 0\text{ N}$. By the First Law, acceleration is zero and the block remains stationary indefinitely.

## Remember

Price momentum in equity markets is the financial analogue of Newton's First Law: an asset trending upward tends to continue rising absent a market-moving shock. The Jegadeesh–Titman (1993) momentum strategy exploits this *price inertia* by going long recent winners and short recent losers over a 3–12 month horizon. An "external force" — an earnings surprise, central bank announcement, or liquidity crisis — is required to reverse the trend, just as Newton's Law requires a non-zero resultant force to change a body's velocity.
