# DV01

## Definition

**DV01** (Dollar Value of a Basis Point, also called PV01 or PVBP) is the monetary change in a bond or portfolio's value for a one-basis-point (0.01%) parallel downward shift in the yield curve. It is the primary risk metric for fixed income trading, expressing interest rate sensitivity in cash terms rather than percentage terms.

## Key Formula

For a bond with price $P$ and yield $y$ (in decimal), DV01 is the finite-difference approximation of the price–yield derivative:

$$\text{DV01} = -\frac{\Delta P}{\Delta y} \times 0.0001$$

where $\Delta P$ is the price change for a one-basis-point move. In terms of **modified duration** $D_{\text{mod}}$:

$$\text{DV01} = D_{\text{mod}} \times P \times 0.0001$$

For a bond with annual coupon $c$, face value $F$, yield $y$, and $n$ periods to maturity, the modified duration is:

$$D_{\text{mod}} = \frac{1}{P} \sum_{t=1}^{n} t \cdot \frac{CF_t}{(1+y)^t}$$

where $CF_t$ is the cash flow at time $t$ (coupon or coupon plus face value).

## Example

A 5-year government bond has a face value of £1,000, a 4% annual coupon, and a current yield of 4.00% (so it prices at par: $P = £1{,}000$). Its modified duration is approximately 4.45 years.

$$\text{DV01} = 4.45 \times 1{,}000 \times 0.0001 = \textbf{£0.445}$$

A one-basis-point fall in yield increases the bond's value by approximately £0.445. For a £10 million position, DV01 = £4,450 — meaning a 10 bp move shifts the P&L by roughly £44,500.

## Remember

DV01 is the core tool for hedging and risk management in fixed income: a trader long £5,000 DV01 in government bonds can hedge by going short the same DV01 in gilt futures. Because DV01 is additive across positions, a portfolio's total interest rate risk is simply the sum of each instrument's DV01 — making it straightforward to set risk limits, compute daily P&L attribution, and size hedges across a rates book.
