## Definition

A **sigma-algebra** (σ-algebra) $\mathcal{F}$ on a sample space $\Omega$ is a collection of subsets of $\Omega$ satisfying three closure properties:

1. $\Omega \in \mathcal{F}$ — the whole space is measurable.
2. If $A \in \mathcal{F}$, then $A^c = \Omega \setminus A \in \mathcal{F}$ — closed under **complement**.
3. If $A_1, A_2, \ldots \in \mathcal{F}$, then $\bigcup_{n=1}^{\infty} A_n \in \mathcal{F}$ — closed under **countable union**.

A subset $A \subseteq \Omega$ is called **measurable** if $A \in \mathcal{F}$. Only measurable sets can be assigned a probability by the measure $\mathbb{P}$. The triple $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, \mathbb{P})$ is a **probability space**.

A **filtration** $\{\mathcal{F}_t\}_{t \geq 0}$ is a family of σ-algebras that is increasing in time:

$$\mathcal{F}_s \subseteq \mathcal{F}_t \quad \text{for all } s \leq t$$

Each $\mathcal{F}_t$ represents the information available at time $t$ — it grows as more outcomes are observed.

## Key Formula

**Generated σ-algebra:** the smallest σ-algebra containing a collection of sets $\mathcal{C}$:

$$\sigma(\mathcal{C}) = \bigcap \{\mathcal{G} : \mathcal{G} \text{ is a } \sigma\text{-algebra and } \mathcal{C} \subseteq \mathcal{G}\}$$

**Natural filtration of a process** $\{X_t\}$: the σ-algebra generated by all observations up to time $t$:

$$\mathcal{F}_t^X = \sigma(X_s : 0 \leq s \leq t)$$

**Measurability of a random variable:** $X$ is $\mathcal{F}$-measurable if and only if:

$$\{X \leq x\} \in \mathcal{F} \quad \text{for all } x \in \mathbb{R}$$

## Example

**Coin toss:** $\Omega = \{H, T\}$. The full power set $\mathcal{F} = \{\emptyset, \{H\}, \{T\}, \{H,T\}\}$ is the only σ-algebra that distinguishes heads from tails. The trivial σ-algebra $\{\emptyset, \Omega\}$ assigns the same probability to everything — it encodes **no information**.

**Two-period binomial tree:** after two steps the sample space is $\Omega = \{HH, HT, TH, TT\}$.

- $\mathcal{F}_0 = \{\emptyset, \Omega\}$ — no information at time 0.
- $\mathcal{F}_1 = \sigma(\{HH, HT\}, \{TH, TT\})$ — we know the outcome of the first toss.
- $\mathcal{F}_2 = \mathcal{P}(\Omega)$ — all outcomes revealed.

The sequence $\mathcal{F}_0 \subseteq \mathcal{F}_1 \subseteq \mathcal{F}_2$ is a filtration representing the arrival of information at each period.

## Remember

Filtrations are the mathematical language for **information flow** in finance. The asset price process $\{S_t\}$ is adapted to its natural filtration $\{\mathcal{F}_t^S\}$, meaning $S_t$ is $\mathcal{F}_t^S$-measurable — today's price is knowable from today's information, but tomorrow's price is not. A **martingale** requires $\mathbb{E}[M_t \mid \mathcal{F}_s] = M_s$: the conditional expectation is taken with respect to a σ-algebra, not merely a set of numbers. Risk-neutral pricing uses the same idea — the no-arbitrage price of a derivative is $V_t = e^{-r(T-t)}\mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}[\text{payoff} \mid \mathcal{F}_t]$, where $\mathcal{F}_t$ encodes everything the market has observed up to time $t$.
