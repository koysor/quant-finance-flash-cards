# Basel III Three Pillars

**Topic:** Banking Regulation
**Tags:** basel iii, minimum capital, supervisory review, disclosure, regulatory capital
**Created:** 2026-04-13
**Author:** Claude Sonnet 4.6

---

## Definition

The Basel III framework structures bank regulation into three mutually reinforcing pillars: Pillar 1 sets minimum quantitative capital requirements for credit, market, and operational risk; Pillar 2 gives supervisors authority to impose additional capital if a bank's specific risk profile is not fully captured by Pillar 1; and Pillar 3 requires public disclosure of risk and capital data so that market participants can impose their own discipline on banks.

## Key Formula

The three pillars interact through the capital requirement:

$$\text{Total Capital Requirement} = \underbrace{\text{Pillar 1 minimum}}_{\text{formulaic}} + \underbrace{\text{Pillar 2 add-on}}_{\text{supervisory judgement}}$$

Under Pillar 1 the minimum total capital ratio is:

$$\frac{\text{Total Regulatory Capital}}{\text{Risk-Weighted Assets}} \geq 8\%$$

including a Capital Conservation Buffer of 2.5%, bringing the effective CET1 minimum to **7%**.

## Example

A bank with RWA of £100 bn must hold at least £4.5 bn CET1 (Pillar 1). The regulator, after reviewing its concentration risk and operational risk culture, adds a Pillar 2A add-on of £1 bn. The bank must therefore hold £5.5 bn CET1, a higher bar than the formulaic minimum. Pillar 3 disclosures then allow investors and analysts to assess whether that capital is sufficient for the bank's specific business model.

## Remember

The three pillars reflect three different disciplinary forces: rules (Pillar 1), supervisors (Pillar 2), and markets (Pillar 3). In practice, Pillar 2 requirements (known as Pillar 2A and Pillar 2G in the UK) are often confidential, meaning that a bank's publicly disclosed capital ratios can look strong while a private Pillar 2 add-on erodes the actual headroom. Quants building internal capital models must ensure their output feeds into the Pillar 2 Internal Capital Adequacy Assessment Process (ICAAP) — the document that justifies a bank's own view of capital adequacy to its regulator.
